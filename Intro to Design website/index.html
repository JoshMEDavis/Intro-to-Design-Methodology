Project 1

Creating the name tag was relatively easy, mostly because the template was already set out for us. After choosing a font and writing my name, the most difficult part of the process was getting the correct file type onto the USB drive. It took me several tries to download a file, and bring it back to the printer, only not to see , it appear on the printer for me to realize that I may have been downloading the wrong type of file. As of the moment, I don’t remember what kind of filed it was being saved as but I realized, with the help of my instructor that I was not downloading, the code needed from Pura slicer in order to print on the machine.

After solving that issue, it was relatively simple to load my filament into the machine, although there was a little bit of a struggle, getting the filament past the sensor part of the loader. The name tag came out just fine, using blue filament initially for the majority of the body and switching to white filament for the lettering and extra details. The printer came out, surprisingly sturdy, and I now use it on my wallet chain.

Whereas some of my classmates chose prints that were specifically made to check the calibration of the 3-D printer, I chose something a bit more simple. The print I downloaded off of Thangs.com, is called tornado, with walls one layer thick, no infill, and a rigid over hanging curve with no supports.

The first print came out as a failure due to me, trying to increase the scale of the print where the first several layers of the printer came out fine, however, at the first major overhang, the lack of support causing the filament to drop ong the plate leading to the printer trying to print filament on the air and creating a mess.

Project 2

Objective of this project was to take pre-existing models and to edit and or combine them into our own creations. This took me several tries and I came up with several designs, until I settled on my idea using four models I got off of things.com. (a cat, a pinecone, a branch for the cat to sit on, and a branch to be used as a stand.)

I edited the assets together on TinkerCad, importing them as STL files. I kept the branches, and the cat practically the same with slight changes to proportions, and I shaped the pinecone to better resemble something called a catkin hanging on the edge of the branch.( That something would cause me great difficulties later while printing).

After merging the model, referred to as CatKin, into a single asset, I decided to duplicate it, and create one a third of the size, and imported both of these into the PuraSlicer software.

While in PuraSlicer I set the print for a 15% infill, and the default .20 mm speed (modified). What I realized while going through the print layer by layer, was that it had three major issues. First was how little of the print actually made contact with the baseboard, there were three main points of contact, which were practically just points, which would easily be picked up or shifted by the extruder and ruined the rest of the print, second was how much of the print was at very horizontal angles, and the printer trying to print on air would either lead to drooping filament, which in the print or filament cling to the extruder, which would break the machine. Lastly was the issue of the free hanging catkins Which had similar issues to the horizontal, but didn’t even have the benefit of more stable structures underneath to support it.

The first issue was easily solved by rotating the models, sinking them a bit, and adding rafts to increase the base’s surface area. The latter issues forced me into my first interaction with support structures. Although PuraSlicer has auto-generated supports, they were on the bottom of every surface and added hours the print time, and the other options just never rendered. Luckily, TA Robert taught me about the paint on support function in Pura. With this the print came out almost perfect, almost. When I painted supports onto the catkins, I only put one point of support on the lowest tip of each catkin expecting it to be enough for the printer to build off of, but I was mistaken. The supports didn't fully form, turning the oblong catkins into spaghetti mess.Going back into PuraSlicer I added more support to the under belly of the overhang, which resulted in the perfect print.
